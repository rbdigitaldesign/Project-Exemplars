LIVE & LOCAL
Designing a Platform to Sustain Adelaide's Independent Live Music Ecosystem
Zoe Park  ·  Daniel Russo  ·  Aisha Okafor  ·  Marcus Webb
Creative Arts Business Information Technology Urban Planning
The Problem
"Adelaide's independent live music ecosystem is structurally disadvantaged by digital platforms optimised for scale — making it harder for emerging artists to build a local audience and for small venues to fill rooms."
The problem is not that audiences cannot find live music. It is that the existing digital infrastructure is designed against the people who need it most.

Why Existing Platforms Fall Short
Algorithms optimised for engagement consistently surface what audiences already know. This reinforces popularity at the expense of discovery — and systematically disadvantages emerging artists who need visibility, not engagement optimisation.
Design Options Considered
A Simple events listing — no curation or ranking ✗
B AI recommendation based on listening history ✗
C Human-AI hybrid discovery platform — AI surfaces underrepresented acts; human curators make editorial judgments ✓
Option B was rejected: AI trained on listening history would replicate the structural bias the project set out to solve.
Who Is Involved
Tech platforms (outer ring) have business models in direct tension with the problem being solved — they benefit from engagement, not discovery.

AI handles the information problem. People handle the taste and values problem.
Editorial independence is structurally protected from commercial pressure. Curation criteria are transparent and publicly published. The platform's explicit design objective is to surface the unfamiliar, not optimise for the already-liked.
Key Risks Identified
Algorithmic bias toward English-language mainstream genres — requires intervention at the data and design level, not just as a policy statement
Platform dependency: building audiences on infrastructure you don't own replicates the structural problem one generation later
Editorial independence needs structural protection — commercial pressure from advertising and sponsorship creates a real conflict of interest

Problem statement
Finding: AI-generated audience personas skewed younger and more digitally confident than evidence supported. Every AI output required critical review and substantial revision.
What We Found
The core problem is structural disadvantage, not poor discoverability — solving discovery without addressing structure changes nothing
Designing an AI system to be intentionally bad at one thing (familiarity) so it can be good at another (discovery) is a fundamentally different design problem
Live music is urban infrastructure — cities with healthy independent scenes attract residents, students, and visitors
There is no commercially obvious revenue model for this platform that does not eventually compromise what makes it worth building
Scope and Limitations
This project produced a service design model and platform blueprint. It does not address venue economics, musician pay rates, or ticketing fees — structural problems that no platform solves. These were identified as assumptions the design carries, not problems it resolves.
What We Would Do Differently
Co-design with musicians and venue owners from day one, not desk research only
Identify revenue model conflicts earlier — we found the advertising tension very late
Agree AI use boundaries upfront rather than deciding as we went
Team Reflections
Zoe Park — Creative Arts "Working out why Spotify's algorithm works the way it does — and who it's actually designed to serve — has genuinely changed how I use it."
Daniel Russo — Business "Every revenue stream we considered created a conflict of interest. There's no commercially obvious way to build this without eventually compromising what makes it worth building."
Aisha Okafor — Information Technology "Designing an AI that is intentionally bad at giving you what you already like, so it can be good at discovery — that was a genuinely new way of thinking about recommendation systems."
Marcus Webb — Urban Planning "Once I started thinking about live music as urban infrastructure rather than entertainment, the stakes of the project felt much higher."
